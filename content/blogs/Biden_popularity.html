Create a plot

We calculate the average net approval rate (approve- disapprove) for each week since President Biden got into office. Then we plot the net approval, along with its 95% confidence interval. We use the ‘enddate’ as date and facet by the subgroup to see the difference between adults, voters and all polls.

# Create confidence levels
approval_margins <- approval_pollist %>%
  
  #Select enddate
  filter(!is.na(enddate)) %>%
  mutate(week=isoweek(enddate)) %>%
  
  #Group the data
  group_by(week, subgroup) %>%
  
  #Summarize data (use se formula for differences)
  summarise(
    number = n(),
    net_approval_rate = mean(adjusted_approve)-mean(adjusted_disapprove),
    sd_approve = sd(adjusted_approve),
    sd_disapprove = sd(adjusted_disapprove),
    se = sqrt((sd_approve)**2/number+((sd_disapprove)**2/number)),
    t_critical=qt(0.975, number- 1 ),
            lower = net_approval_rate- (t_critical*se),
            upper = net_approval_rate+ (t_critical*se))

Analysis

Looking at the confidence interval, we can see that its size greatly differ between week 4 and week 25. One of the reasons for this is that the sample set size differs between week 4 and week 25. The sample size on week 4 is much smaller than that of week 25 which is why the standard error is relatively higher in week 4. This leads to larger confidence intervals in week 4 compared to week 25. As far as the data across the weeks is concerned, as sample size increases - confidence intervals shrink. The approval ratings for Joe Biden have reduced between week 4 and 25.
